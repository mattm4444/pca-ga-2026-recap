# General Assembly 2026 — A Recap for Our Congregation
### A plain-English summary of the 53rd PCA General Assembly — *living draft, updated as results come in*

> **Status:** Built from **Day 1** of the Overtures Committee (Mon., June 22).
> The Assembly meets through **Thursday**. Sections marked **[TO UPDATE]** get
> filled in as the committee finishes and the votes reach the Assembly floor.
> **Important to say out loud:** most of what follows so far is a *committee

## 1. Setting it up for the congregation

Once a year the whole Presbyterian Church in America gathers — pastors and
elders from across the country — for its national family meeting, the General
Assembly. This year it met in Louisville; it was the 53rd.

A lot of that meeting is spent on **overtures** — formal requests from local
groups of churches asking the whole denomination to do something: change a rule,
make a statement, study a question. This year there were **89 of them — the most
in the denomination's history** (the previous record was 63, and 41 of those were
on a single subject). About **82** came to the Overtures Committee; the rest were
routed elsewhere. That number alone tells you something: this is a church with a
great deal it cares about, and a great deal it doesn't yet agree on.

And that is where our passage meets us. *"Behold, how good and pleasant it is
when brothers dwell in unity"* (Psalm 133:1). Set that line beside a room of
faithful people working through 82 contested requests — including the hardest one
of all — and the honest question rises up: **what does unity even mean when
brothers disagree this much? Is it real, or do we just keep quiet to keep the
peace?** That's the tension. Here's what the family actually did with it.

---

## 2. The decision everyone was watching: women and the office of deacon

The single most contested question was whether women may be **ordained as
deacons** — the office of mercy and service. Eleven overtures circled it.

On Day 1 the committee **declined** the central proposal (Overture 37) by a wide
margin — **115 to 14** — holding that our Constitution already speaks
sufficiently about the office. The whole cluster of related overtures fell with
it. **[TO UPDATE: a minority report was expected on the Assembly floor — note
what happened there.]**

Two things are worth saying honestly to the congregation:
- This was **not** a vote about whether women are valued, gifted, or essential to
  the church's ministry of mercy — Scripture and our own Book of Church Order
  already say godly women labor in that ministry.
- It **was** a vote about ordination to office — and faithful, Bible-loving
  believers genuinely disagree about it. That disagreement is the tension our
  passage — Psalm 133 — sets us inside.

## 3. The other decisions that matter to us (Day 1)

In plain English, what the committee recommended on the first day:

- **Thanks and prayer.** Approved a prayer of thanksgiving for the nearly 5,900
  adults who professed faith in our churches last year, a call to a day of prayer,
  and (trimmed down) thanks on the nation's 250th anniversary.
- **A statement on biblical manhood and womanhood (the "Danvers Statement").**
  Affirmed — but by a razor-thin **66 to 64**. Expect this one to be debated again
  on the floor. **[TO UPDATE: floor result.]**
- **Artificial Intelligence.** Approved a small committee to study how AI affects
  churches and families (modest budget, practical focus). A parallel request to
  study Critical Race Theory was **declined**.
- **Caring for the church's integrity.** Strengthened the process by which the
  whole Assembly can step in when a regional body fails to act on a serious matter
  (Overture 27, approved 123–10).
- **Sent back home.** Several requests were referred back to their presbyteries to
  be reworked rather than decided — a normal, healthy way of saying "not yet."

**By the numbers (Day 1):** about **38 of the 82** overtures before the committee
handled — roughly 45%. The rest carried into the following days.

## 4. Beyond the overtures — the wider Assembly

The overtures get the attention, but most of the week is the church simply
*hearing how its ministries are doing.* The picture was largely encouraging:

- **How it opened.** Roughly **2,300 commissioners** gathered. The interim Stated
  Clerk celebrated more than **5,700 professions of faith** across PCA churches
  this past year — and opened by urging the Assembly toward **a spirit of unity
  and charity** in how brothers engage one another — naming the tension out loud
  before a single contested vote. The Assembly elected its Moderator to chair the meeting
  (RE Mel Duncan), and elected a new permanent Stated Clerk, **Fred Greco**, by
  unanimous voice vote.
- **Our schools are healthy.** Encouraging reports from **Covenant Seminary** and
  **Covenant College**, including strong new faculty. One proposal would have
  restricted the teaching of any course touching Scripture or doctrine to ordained
  elders only; both schools and the Assembly **decisively rejected it** — it
  confused a *college* with a *church*. (It never went to the Overtures Committee.)
- **Church planting in transition (MNA).** Mission to North America is between
  coordinators, reporting improved finances and a renewed focus on its core work —
  assessing and training church planters, disaster relief, and ministry in hard
  places. The Assembly also approved **two new presbyteries** (a Smoky Mountain
  Presbytery around Knoxville, and one in Arkansas) — which, worth noting, slightly
  raises the bar for the two-thirds-of-presbyteries ratification votes.
- **The Christian Nationalism study.** The committee gave an *interim* report —
  still "preliminary and incomplete." Its final work will include a pastoral
  letter and a set of affirmations and denials; the Assembly approved sending the
  draft to churches and presbyteries for input while the committee works another
  year.
- **Missions (MTW).** A bright spot: the "1% Challenge" (to send 1% of our members
  to the mission field) was **exceeded — 1.9% actually went.** The coordinator,
  Lloyd Kim, was reelected.
- **The other agencies** — Christian education (CDM), the PCA Foundation, Geneva
  Benefits (retirement, insurance, and relief for pastors' widows and others in
  need), the camp ministry (Ridge Haven), interchurch relations (new ties with
  churches in Australia, Scotland, and Europe), and the review of presbytery
  records — all reported and had their recommendations approved, most without
  controversy.

### [TO UPDATE] The overture floor votes
- **The one formal split among the overtures was *not* the deacon question.** Of
  all 82 overtures, only one produced a minority report: **Overture 43**, on
  whether the denomination's coordinators and Stated Clerk should be elected to
  **four-year terms** instead of the current **annual** election. The committee
  narrowly favored the change (73–53); the minority urged keeping yearly elections
  — "an annual election is not a nuisance; it is one of the ordinary ways the
  courts of the church maintain accountability," and changing a fifty-year pattern
  this fast, without the presbyteries' input, is unwise. It's a fitting picture of
  how this church argues: not over whether to be faithful, but over *how* — and
  even the disagreement is framed in the language of stewardship and care. (This is
  a rule-of-order change, so it takes effect at the Assembly itself — no year-long
  presbytery vote.) **[TO UPDATE: how the floor decided it.]**
