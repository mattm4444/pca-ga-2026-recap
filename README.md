# General Assembly 2026 — A Recap for Our Congregation
### A plain-English summary of the 53rd PCA General Assembly (Louisville) — *living draft, updated as results come in*

> **Worth saying plainly:** much of what follows is still a *committee

## 1. Setting it up: what the General Assembly is

Once a year the whole Presbyterian Church in America gathers — teaching elders
(pastors) and ruling elders (lay elders) sent as *commissioners* from across the
country — for its national family meeting, the General Assembly. This year it met
in Louisville, Kentucky; it was the **53rd**, and roughly **2,300 commissioners**
came.

A great deal of that meeting runs on **overtures.** An overture is a formal
request from a local presbytery — a regional family of churches — asking the
whole denomination to do something: change a rule, make a statement, or study a
question. Most overtures aim at one of two documents: the **Book of Church Order
(BCO)**, which is the church's constitution for how it governs itself, or the
**Rules of Assembly Operations (RAO)**, which govern how the Assembly itself runs.

What makes the PCA distinctive is how deliberately *hard* it is to change
anything. A change to the constitution has to clear three gates: a committee
recommends it, the Assembly adopts it, and then **two-thirds of the presbyteries
(about 58 of 88) ratify it** over the next year. A proposal can win a lopsided
floor vote and still quietly die in the presbyteries months later. That slowness
is on purpose — it is how a confessional church keeps itself from acting on a
passing majority or a single charged moment.

This year set a record. **Eighty-nine overtures** were submitted — by far the
most in the church's history (the previous high was 63, in 2016, and 41 of those
were on the single topic of racial reconciliation; before that, 52 in 2014).
**Eighty-two** came to the Overtures Committee; the rest were routed to other
committees, plus one more — a study committee on Artificial Intelligence — that
carried over from last year. Of the proposals, **54 sought to amend the BCO and
15 the RAO.** The committee had only about **13½ hours** to work through them, and
the full Assembly about **16½ hours** of floor time. So much of the week was
*triage* — deciding which questions deserved full debate and which could be
handled quickly, often by settling one clear overture and letting the related
ones fall with it. One presbytery alone, **Westminster, submitted 17 overtures** —
nearly a fifth of the entire docket.

The sheer volume tells you something true about this church: it is a body with a
great deal it cares about, and a great deal it does not yet agree on. The honest
question underneath the whole week is the one every family knows — **what does
unity actually mean when brothers disagree this deeply? Is it real, or is it just
keeping quiet to keep the peace?** Here is what the family did with that question.

## 2. The decision everyone was watching: women and the office of deacon

By a wide margin the most contested question was the **diaconate** — the church's
office of mercy and practical service — and specifically whether, and how, women
may serve in it. **Eleven overtures** clustered here, and they pulled in opposite
directions: some sought to *open* the office (or at least its title) to women;
others sought to *tighten* the rules and fence the office more firmly for ordained
men.

At the center stood **Overture 37,** which asked the Assembly to amend the BCO so
that a local session could, at its own discretion, ordain women as deacons. On Day
1 the committee **declined it by a wide margin — 115 to 14** — holding that the
Constitution already speaks sufficiently to the question, and the related cluster
of overtures largely fell with it. The church's **Committee on Constitutional
Business** had also reviewed the proposal and found it in conflict with the
constitution. Notably, **no minority report was filed on the deacon question** —
which means it was *not* reopened for a floor fight (the one overture that was, see
§4, had nothing to do with deacons).

Two things are worth saying plainly to the congregation:

- This was **not** a vote about whether women are valued, gifted, or essential to
  the church's ministry of mercy. Scripture and our own Book of Church Order
  already affirm that godly women labor in that ministry — and the Reformed
  tradition has long honored a visible ministry of women in works of mercy
  (Calvin and Hodge both called Phoebe, in Romans 16, a "deaconess").
- It **was** a vote about *ordination to office* — the vows and authority that
  come with it — and that is exactly where faithful, Bible-loving believers
  genuinely disagree. The historic theologians (Calvin, Hodge, Thornwell, Bavinck)
  all agreed the diaconate is a permanent office of *mercy, not rule*; but the line
  each of them drew was an *unordained* order of service, not ordination to the
  constitutional office. That distinction is essentially what the PCA already
  practices today: godly men and women assisting in the ministry of mercy without
  holding the ordained office.

A subtler proposal, **Overture 38,** tried a different route — letting churches
give unordained people the *title* "deacon" through "commissioning" rather than
ordination. It did not survive either. Among the whole cluster, the proposals that
fared best were the ones that simply restated the existing line: affirming
unordained men and women in ministry while declining to invent new church offices.

It is also worth keeping the longer view: no proposal to expand women into ordained
office has won a PCA floor vote in the modern era, and as recently as 2024 the
church moved in the opposite direction. This is settled ground in PCA practice —
which is part of why the debate, though sharp, did not ultimately divide the house.

## 3. The other decisions that matter to us (Day 1)

In plain English, what the committee recommended on the first day:

- **Thanks and prayer.** Approved a prayer of thanksgiving for the **5,656 adults**
  who professed faith in PCA churches in the last reporting year, and a call for a
  **denomination-wide day of prayer and fasting for revival in September 2026.**
  These "starter" items are warm and uncontested on purpose — the Assembly tends to
  begin by giving thanks before it takes up anything hard.
- **The nation's 250th anniversary.** Several overtures proposed an official statement
  of thanksgiving for the United States' 250th. The Assembly's advisors had split over
  *how much to say* — wary of the church adopting a long, contestable
  historical-political declaration as its official voice — and the **trimmed-down**
  version, a simple statement of thanks to God for the liberty the country affords,
  was **adopted.** A small but revealing instance of the church being careful about
  when it speaks in the nation's politics.
- **A statement on biblical manhood and womanhood (the "Danvers Statement," O61) —
  adopted.** The committee had split razor-thin (**66–64**), and the floor mirrored
  that division: substantive debate on both sides, ending in a close (and, by some
  accounts, technologically suspect) vote of **934–841–34 (about 52%)** to declare the
  Danvers Statement biblically faithful. This is a *statement* of conviction, not a
  constitutional change, so it does not go to the presbyteries.
- **Artificial Intelligence vs. Critical Race Theory.** The carried-over request to
  form an **Ad Interim Committee on Artificial Intelligence** (O2025-49) was approved
  **1,175–503–12 (70%)** — clearing the required **two-thirds** and making it a
  **third active study committee**
  alongside the Directory for Public Worship and Christian Nationalism committees. A
  parallel request to study **Critical Race Theory** (Overture 80) was **declined,**
  the Assembly siding with the committee's skepticism about launching yet another
  standing study.
- **Guarding the church's integrity.** Strengthened the process by which the wider
  church can step in when a lower court fails to act on a serious matter
  (**Overture 27, approved 123–10**) — a rare near-unanimous result on a matter of
  church discipline.
- **Sent back home.** A large number of requests — including most of the Westminster
  bloc — were **referred back** to their presbyteries to be reworked rather than
  decided. This is a normal, healthy way for the Assembly to say "not yet," and,
  given only 13½ committee hours, also a way of protecting the church's limited time
  for the questions that most needed full debate. It is not a rejection so much as a
  "bring this back when it's ready."

**By the numbers (Day 1):** about **38 of the 82** overtures before the committee
were handled — roughly 45%. The rest carried into the following days.

## 4. Beyond the overtures — the wider Assembly

The overtures get the attention, but most of the week is the church simply
*hearing how its ministries are doing.* The picture was largely encouraging:

- **How it opened.** Roughly **2,300 commissioners** gathered — registration ran
  ahead of any prior year at this point in the calendar, a record level of interest.
  The interim Stated Clerk celebrated **more than 5,600 new professions of faith**
  across PCA churches and opened by urging the Assembly toward **a spirit of unity
  and charity** in how brothers engage one another — naming the tension out loud
  before a single contested vote was taken. The Assembly elected **RE Mel Duncan**
  as Moderator to chair the meeting, and elected a new permanent **Stated Clerk,
  Fred Greco,** by unanimous voice vote — itself a quiet sign of health, since the
  clerk's office had been led on an interim basis.
- **Our schools are healthy.** Encouraging reports from **Covenant Theological
  Seminary** and **Covenant College,** including strong new faculty. One proposal
  would have restricted the teaching of any course touching Scripture or doctrine to
  ordained elders only; both schools and the Assembly **decisively rejected it** — it
  confused a *college* with a *church.* (That one never reached the Overtures
  Committee.)
- **Church planting in transition (MNA).** Mission to North America is between
  coordinators, reporting improved finances and a renewed focus on its core work —
  assessing and training church planters, disaster relief, and ministry in hard
  places. The Assembly also approved **two new presbyteries:** a **Smoky Mountain
  Presbytery** (dividing Tennessee Valley, around the Knoxville area) and an
  **Arkansas Presbytery** (dividing Covenant). Worth noting: adding presbyteries
  slightly raises the bar for the two-thirds ratification votes on constitutional
  changes — the denominator grows.
- **The Christian Nationalism study.** The committee gave an *interim* report —
  still "preliminary and incomplete." Its final work will include a pastoral letter
  and a set of affirmations and denials; the Assembly approved sending the draft to
  churches and presbyteries for input while the committee works another year. A good
  example of the church's instinct to slow down on a charged cultural question rather
  than rush a statement.
- **Missions (MTW).** A bright spot: the "1% Challenge" (to send 1% of our members to
  the mission field) was **exceeded — 1.9% actually went.** The coordinator, Lloyd
  Kim, was reelected.
- **The other agencies** — Christian education (CDM), the PCA Foundation, Geneva
  Benefits (retirement, insurance, and relief for pastors' widows and others in
  need), the camp ministry (Ridge Haven), interchurch relations (new ties with
  churches in Australia, Scotland, and Europe), and the review of presbytery
  records — all reported and had their recommendations approved, most without
  controversy.

### The overture floor votes

The Assembly worked the Overtures Committee report category by category — moving the
lopsided recommendations *in gross* (in a block) and pulling a handful out for
individual debate. What the floor did:

- **Committee-favored overtures (adopted):** 4, 13, 15, 16, 50, 57, 69, 72 (plus O27,
  below). Of these, **4 and 50** were thanksgiving/prayer items, and the
  **250th-anniversary thanksgiving passed as Overture 66** (with the duplicate
  versions, 76 and 89, answered by reference to it). The constitutional pieces are
  flagged below.
- **Overture 27 (original jurisdiction) — adopted.** Pulled from the block for
  spirited debate; this heavily amended **BCO** change lets the General Assembly
  assume original jurisdiction over a matter in a presbytery. Approved, and now heads
  to the presbyteries.
- **Answered "no," closer committee votes:** the Assembly sided with the committee
  against **1, 28, 35.**
- **Answered "no" by wide margin (20 overtures):** 2, 3, 14, 29, 34, 36, 37, 38, 39,
  46, 48, 52, 54, 56, 65, 67, 71, 74, 75, 86 — all sustained. Four were pulled out for
  some biblical-and-historical debate on **women serving in diaconal roles,** but the
  negative recommendation held across the board. **This is where the diaconate
  question (Overture 37) was settled** — no minority report, the cluster fell, the
  committee's "no" stood.
- **Overture 43 (four-year terms) — defeated, 1104–645–13 (63%).** The lone minority
  report. It proposed four-year terms for the Stated Clerk and the Coordinators of MNA,
  MTW, CDM, and RUF in place of the current one-year reelection cycle. The minority
  position — keep the one-year tenure — prevailed and became the main motion. As a
  rule-of-order (RAO) change the four-year version needed a supermajority (about
  two-thirds) it fell short of at 63%.
- **Referred back to presbyteries:** **12, 51, 59** on the merits, plus **22 more**
  that simply ran out of priority time (17–26, 30, 31, 49, 55, 58, 60, 64, 68, 82, 84,
  85, 90). A healthy signal that in a "surge" year the Assembly can set lower-priority
  overtures aside.
- **RAO overtures, in gross:** referred back (5, 8, 32, 63); answered no (6, 7, 9, 33,
  45, 47, 53); adopted (11, 78, 79, 81). The adopted RAO changes needed a supermajority
  of registered commissioners — at least **1,232 votes** — and cleared it easily,
  **1,326–14–2 (99%).**

**BCO amendments now headed to the presbyteries** (need two-thirds — about 60 of 89 —
to ratify within the year): **13, 15, 16, 27, 57, 69, 72.** (RAO changes like 11, 78,
and 81 are final at the Assembly and need no presbytery vote.)

### Other actions

- **GA registration fees — back to parity.** The recent discount for ruling elders was
  ended, restoring an equal fee for teaching and ruling elders. Next year's fee was
  set, and future adjustments tied to inflation indices.
- **Study committees extended.** Both committees approved last year — the **Directory
  for Public Worship** and **Christian Nationalism** — were continued for another year;
  a motion to add new members to them was set aside. (With the new AI committee, three
  study committees are now active.)
